The Brief

Building a company in 2026 looks nothing like it did five years ago. Capital is patient, but users are not. Distribution is cheap, but attention is luxury. The founders winning right now are the ones treating AI as a co‑founder, shipping weekly, and refusing to scale teams before they scale judgment. Here’s the shortlist.

The Five Moves

01

Build with AI, not around it

Treat every hire like a multiplier for agents.

The leanest 2026 companies run on ten humans and a hundred agents. Design the org chart assuming autonomy at every node — if a role can’t compound with AI, rethink whether it belongs in the first five hires.

02

Distribute before you perfect

Ship weekly, in public, with zero shame.

Polish is a feature of mature products, not new ones. The founders compounding attention right now publish raw changelogs, demo ugly prototypes, and treat their audience as the first hiring pool, investor base, and QA team — all at once.

03

Capital is cheap, conviction isn’t

Raise less. Raise slower. Raise on pull.

Fundraising has flipped from performance art back to a quiet transaction. Wait for inbound interest driven by real traction, and size rounds to the milestones you can actually ship in six months — not the vanity targets in the deck.

04

Own your distribution channel

A platform can change the rules overnight.

Rented reach is a liability. Build direct muscle — an email list, a community, a first‑party data loop — so that when an algorithm changes or an ad auction melts, you still have a line of sight to every user who matters.

05

Protect your decision speed

Kill meetings. Write more. Decide faster.

The real moat in an AI‑saturated market is how quickly a small team can read a signal and act. Replace status meetings with written updates, set a 48‑hour rule on all non‑reversible calls, and treat latency as a bug in the company itself.
